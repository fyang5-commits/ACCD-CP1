Scene Documentation: Living Room + Health Dashboard

For this part of the project, I created a cozy living room scene with a tablet on a coffee table. The tablet screen shows my doctor schedule dashboard UI. The idea is that a user can sit at home, open the dashboard, and book an appointment any time, no clinic phone call, no waiting on hold.

The visual direction came from browsing interior display photos on Target’s website. I liked how those product shots staged furniture, plants, and decor in calm lighting. That inspired me to build a space that feels warm and lived-in, not like a tech demo. The focal point became the round table, the plant, and the tablet.

I tried to model a lamp from scratch at first, but I got stuck on the vertex merge and extrude steps. The geometry wasn’t doing what I expected, so I ended up pulling a lamp asset from BlenderKit instead. That let me keep moving and focus on layout, materials, and lighting instead of getting completely blocked on hard-surface modeling. I do want to come back and practice that more.

For lighting, I used soft area lights to fake indoor bounce lighting and keep the shadows gentle. I set the camera at a low, eye-level angle so the viewer feels like they’re actually in the room, looking across the table at the tablet. This angle also shows off the table material, the plant, and the woven textures in the scene.

Overall, this scene helped me understand composition, mood, and storytelling, not just “make an object.” I also learned it’s okay to mix my own work with library assets if it helps communicate the idea clearly on deadline.

Reading Response Week 6

This week’s readings both explored how design is evolving—from system-based thinking and coding in visual design to the role of prototypes as tools for learning. I chose these quotes because they connect to how I’m currently growing as a designer who blends visual craft with technical and strategic thinking.

Reading 1: “Systems, Code, and Design”

Quote 1

“We are now in transition from an object-oriented to a systems-oriented culture. Here change emanates, not from things, but from the way things are done.”
— Jack Burnham (1968), Systems Esthetics

This quote hits because it describes the exact shift we’re living through as designers. Design isn’t just about making a single object look good anymore, it’s about shaping the rules, flows, behaviors, and states around that object. You’re not only designing a thing — you’re designing how it updates, adapts, and connects to everything else over time.

Quote 2

“However, there is a century-long bond between the field of design and new advances in technology, and if graphic designers do not become fluent in this new digital reality, they will become irrelevant. We now have the ability to write code that produces beautiful designs, and the designer of tomorrow will have to understand how to deliver on that promise.”

I like this because it's blunt. The point is: knowing layout, typography, and color is still important, but it’s not enough if you can’t also work in the medium where design now lives (code, systems, motion, interaction). It’s not saying “designers must become engineers,” it’s saying “designers have to stop pretending code is someone else’s job.”

Quote 3

“Throwing a design over the wall for production is a bad legacy of the printed page, and there is no reason for the design process to end with the birth of a product.”

This is calling out the old school “I made the mockup, I’m done” mindset. In digital work, launch is not the end — it’s actually the middle. The product keeps evolving in the hands of real users, and designers should be there for that part too: testing, adjusting, tuning the system, not just handing off a static file.

Reading 2: “What Do Prototypes Prototype?”

This reading helped me rethink what a prototype actually is. I used to see prototypes as “mini versions” of the final design, but this article showed how they’re really learning tools that help explore ideas, test assumptions, and spark conversations. These quotes stood out to me the most because they connect directly to how I approach UX work.

Quote 1

“A prototype is a manifestation of design ideas that allows designers to explore and communicate what could be.”

I love this quote because it defines prototypes as thinking tools, not just deliverables. They make invisible ideas visible and help teams learn faster. It reminds me that even low-fidelity prototypes are powerful because they turn abstract thoughts into shared experiences.

Quote 2

“Prototypes do not have to work—they have to work enough to answer questions.”

This one really changed how I see perfection in design. A prototype isn’t supposed to be finished—it’s supposed to help you learn. Sometimes rough edges are exactly what keep you focused on the right problem instead of polishing details too early.

Quote 3

“The fidelity of a prototype should be determined by what you want to learn from it.”

This makes so much sense in UX work. I used to think higher fidelity was always better, but now I realize it depends on the question. If I’m testing flow, I don’t need pixel-perfect visuals; if I’m testing visual hierarchy, then fidelity matters more. It’s all about aligning effort with learning goals.

Personal Reflection

While I was creating this scene, I got a little carried away with the interior design side and didn’t really think about the deeper purpose of my prototype. After reading the article, I realized I should focus more on the scenario, the users, and the interactions — not just making a beautiful scene. In a way, my project showed me the opposite of what the reading was teaching.
